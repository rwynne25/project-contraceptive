Introduction¶

For this data analysis project, I located two seemingly disparate data sets from GapMinder World. Each data set individually tracked two different trends in various global countries across several decades. One data set focused on rates of contraceptive use in women in various countries from the 1960s to present dat. The other data set contained information on primary school completion rates among women in various global countries from the 1960s to present day.

I surmised that it was very likely that as countries across the globe shoed increased rates of primary school completion among women, that rates of cintraceptive use would also increase in a similar pattern. Knowledge is can be very empowering to women, and as they are more educated, they are likely to choose contraception as a way to further empower their individual lives.

Conclusions¶

According to histograms plotted above, it does appear that rates of primary school completion among women globally do in fact correlate with percentage rates of contraceptive use among women globally. Both histograms plotted above follow a normal distribution pattern.

As I had hypothesized when starting out with these individual data sets acquired from GapMinder World, the data does support my speculation that as women become more empowered by their educations, they are more likely to use contraceptives. According to histograms plotted above, it does appear that rates of primary school completion among women globally do in fact correlate with percentage rates of contraceptive use among women globally. Both histograms plotted above follow a normal distribution pattern.

Limitations¶

One pertinent point that should be considered when viewing these analyses I have created among the two disparate data sets is the fact that for both sets, particularly the data tied to contraceptive rates, there are an inordinate number of null values within the data. My methodology was to fill those values in with the median value of the overal data set for each. However, it can be noted plainly within the visualizations created that there is a marked clustering effect at the median point of the data within each visualization.

These visualizations do not seem to be limited by a small population, as they are based on statistics from within a number of different countries globally over several decades. One potential limition, however, might be the question of whether this data is actually an accurate representation of the population. There is no imformation available on this data set that would speak to the data collection methodology, who was targeted for data collection, when or why.
